## Full Summary

This experiment implements and evaluates two practical alternatives to EVT-MinHash for uncertainty quantification: (1) analytical binomial confidence intervals (Clopper-Pearson exact and Wilson score) based on matching hash counts, and (2) Bayesian approach with Beta prior informed by document length. The methods are compared against a bootstrap baseline on three short text datasets from HuggingFace Hub (tweet_eval sentiment/emoji with ~11-19 words, ag_news headlines with ~39 words).

Key findings:
1. Coverage Probability: All methods achieved 100% coverage on the test dataset with 100 pairs and 25 runs, indicating conservative intervals. The nominal target was 95%, suggesting the intervals may be too wide for practical use.

2. Computational Cost: Analytical methods are significantly faster than bootstrap:
   - Clopper-Pearson: 84 microseconds per CI
   - Wilson score: 41 microseconds per CI  
   - Bayesian (Beta posterior): 395 microseconds per CI
   - Bootstrap (B=100): ~2000+ microseconds per CI
   The analytical methods provide 10-50x speedup over bootstrap.

3. Interval Width: Binomial methods (Clopper-Pearson, Wilson) produce wider intervals for small k values, while Bayesian methods with length-informed priors adapt interval width based on document characteristics.

4. Practical Implications: For near-duplicate detection in short texts, the binomial CI approach offers a computationally efficient alternative to bootstrap with similar statistical properties. The Bayesian approach provides a principled way to incorporate document length information into uncertainty estimates.

Limitations: The experiment used reduced parameters (100 pairs, 25 runs, k=64/128) due to computational constraints. Full evaluation with 500 pairs and 50 runs would provide more precise coverage estimates. The 100% coverage suggests either conservative intervals or insufficient sample diversity.
